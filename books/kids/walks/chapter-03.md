# Walks

## Chapter 3 — The Nose Knows (And Right Now, It Is Worried)

Let me tell you something important about smells.

There are good smells. *Great* smells. *Unbelievably magnificent* smells.

Bacon. Cheeseburgers on a grill far away. The spot outside Mrs. Patterson's house. The mailbox on the corner. Rain on warm pavement. Big Steve's jacket pocket where he sometimes keeps treats. (He thinks I don't know. I always know.)

I could talk about good smells all day. I have organized them. I have ranked them. I have a system.

But this is not a chapter about good smells.

---

We had walked three whole blocks down the new street.

Three blocks! Brand new! Every single thing a surprise!

There was a fire hydrant I had never smelled before. It was very informative. I spent a while on that one.

There was a garden with something in the dirt that made my nose go absolutely wild. I don't know what it was. I don't know if I will ever know. But it was a very big deal.

There was a little piece of pretzel on the sidewalk that I almost got before Big Steve made that sound — the sound that means *leave it* — and I left it. (I thought about it for a whole block afterward. Probably still warm. A real tragedy.)

Everything was wonderful. Everything was new. My tail was going like crazy, back and forth, because that is what my tail does when everything is good and right and perfect.

And then.

**And then.**

My nose stopped.

Not me. Just my nose.

My paws kept walking for one more step, but my nose said: *No. Wait. Stop.*

So I stopped. All of me, this time.

Big Steve looked back at me. "You okay, buddy?"

Buddy. That's me. But even hearing my favorite name did not stop what was happening in my nose.

Because there it was.

A smell.

A smell I did not recognize. A smell I had never smelled before on any walk, on any street, on any morning ever in my whole entire life.

Most new smells make me feel excited. *Ooh! What is that? Let me smell it more! Let me smell it a hundred times!*

This smell did not make me feel excited.

This smell made the fur on the back of my neck stand up — just a little — like a whisper I couldn't quite hear.

It made my ears go flat, then straight up again, then flat again.

It made my tail go very, very still.

I took one careful sniff. Then another.

*Something is here,* my nose told me. *Something I do not know.*

*Something... big.*

I looked down the street. I looked at the shadows near the hedge. I looked behind the old wooden fence with the gap at the bottom just big enough for a nose to sniff through.

I couldn't see anything.

But I could smell it.

And whatever it was, it was close.

Big Steve knelt down next to me. He scratched behind my left ear — the really good spot — and said, "Come on, silly. Nothing there."

He couldn't smell it. Of course he couldn't. He is Big Steve, and he is the best, but his nose is not what I would call a *professional* nose.

My nose is a professional nose.

And my professional nose had something very specific to say.

*Be careful.*

I stayed close to Big Steve's leg for the rest of the walk. Closer than usual. My nose kept working, kept reading the air, kept trying to understand.

But the smell stayed a mystery.

And here is the thing about smells that are mysteries:

They mean something happened here.

Something *was* here.

Maybe something that is going to happen again.

My nose knew it. My tail knew it. My fur knew it.

And when we got back home and I walked through the door and Big Steve took off my signal and said, "Good walk, buddy!" —

I sat down in the hallway.

And I thought about that smell for a very long time.
